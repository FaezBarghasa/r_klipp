# Hardware-In-the-Loop (HIL) Testing Plan

This document outlines the procedures for conducting Hardware-In-the-Loop (HIL) tests on the `r_klipp` firmware. HIL testing is critical for validating the firmware's interaction with real-world hardware, measuring its real-time performance characteristics, and tuning vibration control models.

---

## Section 1: Setup

### Required Hardware

*   **Target MCU Board**: An MKS SKIPR board (STM32F407VET6) or another supported board, flashed with a dedicated HIL test firmware binary.
*   **Logic Analyzer**: A multi-channel logic analyzer capable of at least 24MHz sampling rate (e.g., Saleae Logic 8, DSLogic). Sufficient channels to capture STEP, DIR, and communication pins simultaneously.
*   **Oscilloscope**: A 2-channel digital storage oscilloscope (DSO) with at least 50MHz bandwidth. Useful for analyzing signal integrity and jitter in finer detail.
*   **Current Probe**: A DC current probe or a high-precision shunt resistor with a differential probe for measuring motor and heater current draw.
*   **External Thermocouple/Thermometer**: A calibrated K-type thermocouple with a MAX31855/MAX6675 breakout board, or a high-accuracy infrared thermometer.
*   **Host Computer**: A PC running Linux, with the logic analyzer/oscilloscope software and a serial terminal program.
*   **SPI Accelerometer**: ADXL345 or similar accelerometer connected to the target board via SPI, used for vibration resonance measurements.

### Wiring

1. Connect the logic analyzer's input channels to the STEP and DIR pins of at least two stepper motor drivers (e.g., X and Y).
2. Connect another logic analyzer channel to a GPIO pin that the test firmware will use as a "command received" trigger.
3. Connect the oscilloscope probe to one of the STEP pins for jitter analysis.
4. Attach the external thermocouple to the heater block, adjacent to the printer's own thermistor.
5. Securely mount the accelerometer to the print toolhead or bed, and connect its SPI lines (MOSI, MISO, SCK, CS) to the target MCU SPI header.

## Section 2: Test Procedures

### Test 1: Step Timing Accuracy and Jitter

*   **Objective**: Verify that the time between step pulses generated by the MCU exactly matches the `interval_ticks` commanded by the planner and measure the jitter.
*   **Procedure**:
    1. Create a special test firmware that executes a pre-programmed move with a constant cruise velocity (i.e., all `StepSegment` elements have the same `interval_ticks`). For example, a move of 10,000 steps with an interval of 5000 ticks (20,000 steps/sec at 100MHz).
    2. Start a capture with the logic analyzer on the STEP pin.
    3. Power on the MCU to run the test firmware.
    4. Stop the capture after the move is complete.
*   **Analysis**:
    1. Using the logic analyzer software, place markers on a long sequence of rising edges of the step pulses.
    2. Measure the time between consecutive pulses. This should be consistent and equal to `interval_ticks / CLOCK_FREQ`. For our example, 5000 / 100,000,000 = 50µs.
    3. Calculate the standard deviation of the period. This value is the jitter. For a real-time system, this should be extremely low, ideally less than 1µs.

### Test 2: Thermal Stability and PID/MPC Performance

*   **Objective**: Evaluate the performance of the Model Predictive Control (`MpcThermalEngine`) controller by monitoring its ability to reach and maintain a target temperature, and to verify that the reported temperature matches a calibrated external sensor.
*   **Procedure**:
    1. Flash the standard `r_klipp` firmware to the MCU.
    2. Connect the external thermocouple to the heater block.
    3. Connect the host and issue a command to heat the hotend to 210°C (`SET_HEATER_TEMPERATURE HEATER=extruder TARGET=210`).
    4. Log the temperature data from both the printer's thermistor (via Klipper's status report) and the external thermocouple simultaneously over a period of 5-10 minutes.
    5. Issue a command to turn on the part cooling fan to 100% to simulate a thermal disturbance.
*   **Analysis**:
    1. Plot both temperature series over time.
    2. **Accuracy**: Check the difference between the steady-state temperature reported by the firmware and the external thermocouple (should be within ±2°C).
    3. **Overshoot**: Measure the amount the temperature exceeds the 210°C target on the initial heat-up.
    4. **Stability**: Once at temperature, measure the peak-to-peak fluctuation. This should be less than ±1°C.
    5. **Disturbance Rejection**: Analyze how the temperature drops when the fan turns on and how quickly the MPC controller recovers to the setpoint.

### Test 3: End-to-End Latency

*   **Objective**: Measure the time from the moment a G-code command is sent from the host to the first corresponding step pulse being generated by the MCU.
*   **Procedure**:
    1. Create a test firmware that sets a specific GPIO pin (`TRIGGER_PIN`) high as the very first action inside the UART receive task when a specific, known command is received.
    2. Connect the logic analyzer to the host's UART TX pin, the MCU's `TRIGGER_PIN`, and the X-axis STEP pin.
    3. Set the logic analyzer to trigger on the falling edge of the last byte of the G1 command sent over UART.
    4. Send a simple `G1 X10 F6000` command from the host.
*   **Analysis**:
    1. Measure the time delta T1 between the trigger (end of UART command) and the rising edge of the `TRIGGER_PIN`. This is the MCU's internal command processing latency.
    2. Measure the time delta T2 between the rising edge of the `TRIGGER_PIN` and the first pulse on the STEP pin. This is the motion planning and step generation latency.
    3. The total end-to-end latency is T1 + T2.

### Test 4: Input Shaper Resonance & Vibration Calibration

*   **Objective**: Verify the host-side `InputShaperCalibrator` resonance profiling algorithms and shape output coefficients against physical accelerometer vibration samples.
*   **Procedure**:
    1. Execute a test motion sweep command (e.g. `SHAPER_CALIBRATE AXIS=X`) to oscillate the print head from 10 Hz to 150 Hz.
    2. Read raw accelerometer measurements via the SPI sensor loop at a stable rate of 3.2 kHz.
    3. Process the collected acceleration samples through the `InputShaperCalibrator` in [hil_analyzer.rs](file:///home/jrad/RustroverProjects/r_klipp-workspace/r_klipp/crates/klipper-host/src/hil_analyzer.rs).
*   **Mathematical Analysis & Solvers**:
    1. **Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)**: The calibrator computes the DFT magnitude spectrum of the sampled data:
       $$\text{DFT}[k] = \sqrt{\left(\sum x_n \cos\left(\frac{2\pi k n}{N}\right)\right)^2 + \left(\sum x_n \sin\left(\frac{2\pi k n}{N}\right)\right)^2}$$
    2. **Dominant Frequency Detection**: Scans the DFT output peaks to extract the structural resonance frequency:
       $$f_{dominant} = \frac{k_{max} \cdot f_{sample}}{N}$$
    3. **Zero-Vibration (ZV) Shaper Solver**: Solves the ZV impulse scaling factor ($K$) and time delay ($T_d$):
       $$d = e^{\frac{-\zeta \pi}{\sqrt{1 - \zeta^2}}}$$
       $$K_{zv} = \frac{1}{1 + d}, \quad T_{zv} = \frac{1}{2 f_{dominant}}$$
    4. **Minimum Zero-Vibration (MZV) Shaper Solver**: Solves the three MZV impulse amplitudes ($A_1, A_2, A_3$) and damped period ($T_d$):
       $$k = e^{\frac{-\zeta \pi}{\sqrt{1 - \zeta^2}}}$$
       $$A_1 = \frac{1}{(1 + k)^2}, \quad A_2 = \frac{2 k}{(1 + k)^2}, \quad A_3 = \frac{k^2}{(1 + k)^2}, \quad T_{d} = \frac{1}{f_{dominant}}$$
    
    The resulting impulse filters are applied to the Kinematic Planner to shape input step sequences and eliminate mechanical resonance.
